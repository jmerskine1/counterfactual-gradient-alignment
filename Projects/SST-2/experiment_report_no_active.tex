\section{Introduction}
This report summarizes the experiments performed on the \textbf{SST-2} dataset with \textbf{Active Learning disabled}. The goal was to see how CGA performs on purely random data subsets.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\textbf{Dataset:} SST-2 (Binary Sentiment). \\
\textbf{Active Learning:} Disabled. \\
\textbf{Variables:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Train Set Size:} \{10, 20, 50, 100\} samples (randomly selected).
    \item \textbf{Mixing Alpha (\(\alpha\)):} \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Observations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stable Performance:} The results were nearly identical across different training sizes, likely due to the small data regime and random selection leading to similar subset properties.
    \item \textbf{Baseline Dominance:} Without active learning to pick informative points, the standard Cross Entropy baseline outperformed all directional loss variants.
    \item \textbf{Informative Samples:} This suggests that the quality of samples (as provided by active learning) is a critical factor for CGA to provide a benefit.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
On SST-2, CGA's benefits are strongly coupled with active learning. With purely random data selection, CGA did not show an improvement over the baseline.
